About This Project

Public transportation is not distributed equitably. This project compares demographic vulnerability against subway and metro networks in New York City and Washington, D.C. The choropleth maps show where high-need communities live, overlaid with the actual transit network. The bar charts measure the disparity in average distance to the nearest subway and metro networks between minority-majority and non-minority neighborhoods. Together, these visualizations reveal where future transit investment would have the greatest equity impact.

The Vulnerability Index is a composite score built from two components: Racial minority population share (60% weight) reflects historically underserved communities; and Senior citizen concentration (40% weight) accounts for populations with reduced mobility who depend most on public transit. These weights reflect deliberate prioritization, as race is the strongest predictor of transit inequity in U.S. cities.

Key Findings

Washington D.C. shows a significantly larger transit equity gap (+23%) than New York City (+5%). Minority-majority census tracts in D.C. are on average 0.20 km farther from a Metro station than non-minority tracts, compared to only a 0.05 km gap in NYC. This difference likely reflects D.C.'s more spatially segregated layout and the historically limited Metro coverage east of the Anacostia River.

In NYC, the vulnerability index reveals the Bronx and parts of northern Brooklyn as the highest-need areas. Despite high vulnerability scores, these neighborhoods benefit from relatively dense subway coverage, which helps explain why the overall equity gap is smaller. The subway's grid-like density in the outer boroughs partially offsets the disparity.

In D.C., Wards 7 and 8 in the eastern part of the city show the highest vulnerability scores and are the furthest from Metro stations. This reflects a well-documented pattern of underinvestment east of the Anacostia River, where majority-Black communities have historically had fewer transit options despite being among the city's most transit-dependent populations.

The cross-city average equity gap of ~14% suggests that even in cities with robust transit systems, minority and lower-income communities face measurably worse access. This gap compounds other disadvantages such as longer commutes, higher transportation costs, and reduced access to jobs and services.

Limitations & Future Work

The vulnerability index currently incorporates racial minority population share (60%) and senior citizen concentration (40%), but lacks poverty data because the DP05 ACS dataset does not include sufficient economic characteristics. Incorporating DP03 data in a future iteration would produce a more complete composite score, particularly for NYC where economic inequality varies significantly across boroughs.

Distance to the nearest station is a simplified proxy for transit access. It does not account for service frequency, hours of operation, travel time to key destinations, or first/last mile connectivity. A neighborhood 0.8 km from a station that runs every 20 minutes may have worse effective access than one 1.2 km from a station with 2-minute headways.

This analysis uses 2019 ACS data for NYC and 2018 data for D.C. Post-pandemic service cuts, ridership shifts, and new infrastructure such as the NYC Second Avenue Subway extension are not reflected in these results.
